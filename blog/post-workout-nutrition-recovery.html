Post-Workout Nutrition: How to Recover Better

By SH Fitness Tools Team Published 16 February 2026 5 min read

Post-workout nutrition has a reputation for being urgent and precise. In reality, it's a lot more forgiving than the supplement industry suggests.

On this page

  1. The 'anabolic window' is wider than you think
  2. What actually helps recovery
  3. Simple post-workout options

The 'anabolic window' is wider than you think

The idea that you have a narrow 30-minute window after training to eat protein or lose your gains isn't well supported. Research generally shows that as long as you get adequate protein across the day, having a meal within a couple of hours of training is more than sufficient — there's no cliff edge where the benefit disappears. This is genuinely good news: it means you don't need to panic if you can't eat immediately after a session.

What actually helps recovery

Protein remains the priority, providing the amino acids needed for muscle repair — aim for roughly 0.3-0.4g per kg of bodyweight in your post-training meal, in line with your overall daily target from the Protein Intake Calculator. Carbohydrates help replenish glycogen stores, which matters more if you're training again within 24 hours, and are less critical if your next session is a few days away.

Simple post-workout options

You don't need a specialised recovery shake — a normal meal with a protein source and some carbohydrate covers the same ground for most people. If you're short on time, a protein shake with a piece of fruit is a fine bridge option before a full meal later. Consistency across your training week matters far more than optimising any single post-workout meal down to the minute.
